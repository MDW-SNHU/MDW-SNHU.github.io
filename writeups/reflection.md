# **Professional Self‑Assessment**

## **Overview**
Over the years I’ve spent in the Computer Science program at SNHU, I’ve grown from someone who could write code into someone who can design, build, debug, and deliver complete systems. This capstone project brought that growth into focus. What started years ago as a small CRUD module in CS340 eventually became a full stack MongoDB management environment with a web interface, a SQL‑to‑Mongo translation engine, and a complete database management layer. Working through the capstone reminded me how much I’ve learned, how far I’ve come, and how ready I am to take these skills into a professional environment.

## **Growth Through the Capstone**
One of the biggest areas of growth for me has been in algorithmic thinking and problem decomposition. The SQL translation engine was the clearest example of that. Translating SQL into MongoDB operations meant breaking down commands, preserving quoted strings, identifying keywords, and routing everything to the right handler. Adding JOIN support pushed me even further, since MongoDB doesn’t have a direct equivalent. I had to rethink the problem, explore aggregation pipelines, and design something that made sense in a document‑oriented world. That work strengthened my understanding of both SQL and MongoDB and gave me a deeper appreciation for how different database models approach the same problems.

Throughout the project, I made a conscious effort to follow **Python’s PEP‑8 standards** and general clean‑code practices. That meant writing functions with clear purposes, naming things consistently, spacing code for readability, and keeping modules modular. FastAPI and Pydantic naturally encourage this kind of structure, and the more I worked with them, the more I appreciated how much easier development becomes when you follow established patterns instead of fighting them. These habits are the same ones used in professional engineering teams, and they’re skills I’ll carry forward into any future role.

The capstone also gave me plenty of opportunities to deal with real‑world engineering challenges. Implementing HTTPS with Uvicorn required generating certificates, wiring them into the startup logic, and making sure the server behaved correctly whether certificates were present or not. FastAPI and Swagger had their quirks too — dynamic response types, schema mismatches, and UI oddities that didn’t always behave the way I expected. Debugging those issues meant digging into documentation, experimenting with different approaches, and sometimes rewriting parts of the code to keep the interface stable. These weren’t just academic exercises; they were the kinds of problems developers face every day in production systems.

Communication was another major part of the project. Each milestone required a written narrative and an audio explanation, and the Swagger UI itself is a form of communication. It documents every endpoint automatically, shows example inputs, and makes the system approachable even for someone who has never touched MongoDB before. Writing the narratives helped me reflect on the work, explain my design choices, and present the project in a way that made sense to a wide audience. Those communication skills are just as important in the workplace as the technical ones.

Even though the development itself was a solo effort, the final system supports collaborative learning. The SQL helper makes the tool accessible to users who may not have prior experience with MongoDB, and the interface is structured so that beginners and advanced users can both get something out of it. That aligns with the program outcome of supporting diverse audiences and enabling informed decision‑making. It also reflects the kind of environment I want to contribute to professionally — one where tools help people learn, explore, and work together.

- Designing solutions with algorithmic reasoning

These skills reflect not just what I learned in the capstone, but what I’ve built up over my entire time at SNHU.

## **Closing Statement**
This capstone project brought everything full circle. It challenged me, taught me new things, and confirmed that I’m ready for the next step. More importantly, it reminded me why I enjoy this field — solving problems, building tools, and learning something new every time I sit down to write code.

The skills I’ve gained — Python development, API design, database management, debugging, clean coding, and system integration — are exactly the kinds of skills employers look for in software engineering, backend development, DevOps, and data‑focused roles. I’m comfortable working with modern tools, I understand how to structure and maintain real systems, and I’ve learned how to push through the challenges that come with building something from scratch.

This project is something I’m proud to show to prospective employers, and it represents the kind of thoughtful, practical work I want to keep doing in my career.
